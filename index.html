Scoring methodology
Eight signals. One transparent score.

No black box. Every point in the final score traces back to a named signal you can inspect, weight, and defend. Click any signal to see how it's calculated.

S1 · 0–40 PTS
Registration status
Active / suspended / cancelled / suo-moto
The single heaviest signal. A suo-moto cancellation by the tax department scores 40 points outright and forces a floor of 90 on the final score — this overrides every other signal because regulator-initiated cancellation is the strongest possible red flag.
S2 · 0–25 PTS
Filing gap (Y1/Y2/Y3)
Time-weighted across 3 years
Measures missed GSTR-3B filings against expected count, split into Y1 (last 12mo), Y2, and Y3 bands. Weighted 50/30/20 — a gap this year hurts far more than a gap three years ago, just like a real credit model.
S3 · 0–20 PTS
Compliance category
GSTN's own risk flag
Inherits GSTN's own internal compliance tagging (black/red/orange/yellow categories) where available, adding 5–20 points depending on severity. This folds the regulator's own assessment into the score rather than ignoring it.
S4 · −3 TO +10 PTS
Filing trajectory
Improving, stable, worsening, stopped
Compares year-over-year filing rate change. A vendor improving from 60% to 90% compliance gets a small bonus (−3 pts); one that's stopped filing entirely after a clean history gets flagged hardest (+10 pts) — sudden stops matter more than gradual decline.
S5 · 0–12 PTS
Filing discipline
Actual days-late, not just on-time/late
Filed 25 days late scores differently than filed 200 days late — most tools treat both as simply "late." Also detects batch-filing patterns, where four or more returns get filed in the same month, a tell that a vendor caught up all at once rather than filing on a normal cadence.
S6 · 0–5 PTS
Supply divergence
GSTR-1 vs GSTR-3B gap
Flags vendors who declare outward supplies in GSTR-1 but delay the matching tax payment in GSTR-3B — a classic early sign of cash-flow distress. The threshold adjusts automatically for quarterly (QRMP) filers so they aren't falsely flagged.
S7 · 0–5 PTS
E-invoice status
Mandate compliance, conditional
Only applies if the vendor is already trending risky (base score above 30) and is mandated for e-invoicing but non-compliant. This signal acts as a confirming flag rather than a primary driver — it doesn't punish a vendor on its own.
S8 · 0–8 PTS
New entity flag
Registration age under 6 months
Newer entities have less filing history to evaluate, which is itself a risk factor for large exposure decisions. Entities under 3 months old get 8 points; 3–6 months get 5. This decays to zero past 6 months as real history accumulates.

Why it's different
Four things a manual CA review can't do

Compliance checks tell you what happened. This tells you what's likely to happen next.

Temporal risk modeling
Most compliance tools give a single today/not-today flag. This engine splits 36 months of filing history into Y1/Y2/Y3 bands and weights recent behavior far more heavily — a default this year moves the score more than the same default three years ago.
Y1 · Y2 · Y3 weighted bands
Divergence detection
A vendor can look compliant on paper while quietly delaying tax payment behind declared sales. This engine cross-references GSTR-1 against GSTR-3B specifically to catch that gap — the clearest early signal of cash-flow distress hiding inside GST data.
S6 · GSTR-1 vs GSTR-3B
QRMP-aware baselining
Quarterly filers shouldn't be judged against a monthly cadence. The engine auto-detects QRMP status and adjusts the expected filing count from 12 to 4 per year — this alone prevents the false "low compliance" flags that punish smaller MSMEs unfairly.
Auto-adjusted expectations
Self-healing data parsing
Government APIs return inconsistent formats — month names instead of "MMYYYY," missing fields, odd date orders. The parser normalizes all of it on the fly instead of crashing, so the pipeline keeps running even when the source data is messy.
Resilient by design
